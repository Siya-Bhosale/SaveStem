The Miracle of Stem Cells

They are the body's raw materials — cells from which all other cells with specialized functions are generated.

The Power of Donation

For patients with diseases like leukemia, lymphoma, and thalassemia, a stem cell transplant is more than a treatment; it's a second chance at life. A donation from a matching, unrelated donor can rebuild their entire immune system and offer a cure.

Becoming a donor is a profound act of kindness. The process is safe, and your contribution is immeasurable.

The Promise of Preservation

Banking your baby's umbilical cord blood is a forward-thinking health investment. These powerful stem cells are a perfect genetic match for your child and have a high probability of matching other family members, potentially treating dozens of diseases.

It's a biological safety net, providing peace of mind for your family's future health challenges.

Your Guide to Giving Life

Here’s how you can become a donor or preserve your baby's stem cells.

Donating Stem Cells

  1. 1

    Register Online

    Join a stem cell registry. You'll fill out a form and receive a simple cheek swab kit at home.

  2. 2

    Swab & Send

    Collect a sample of your cheek cells with the swab and mail it back. Your tissue type will be added to the registry.

  3. 3

    Wait for a Match

    If you are a match for a patient, you'll be contacted for further testing.

  4. 4

    Donate & Save a Life

    Most donations are like giving blood (PBSC donation). In rare cases, it involves a minor surgical procedure to collect marrow from the hip bone.

Preserving Cord Blood

  1. 1

    Choose a Cord Blood Bank

    Research and select a reputable public or private cord blood bank before your baby is due.

  2. 2

    Receive a Collection Kit

    The bank will send you a kit to take to the hospital on delivery day.

  3. 3

    Inform Your Doctor

    Let your medical team know you plan to collect the cord blood. The collection is safe and painless for both mother and baby.

  4. 4

    Collection & Storage

    After birth, the cord blood is collected and sent to the bank for processing and long-term cryogenic storage.

Frequently Asked Questions

Most donations (90%) are done through a process similar to donating plasma, called PBSC donation, which is non-surgical. Donors may experience flu-like symptoms. For bone marrow donation, it's a surgical procedure under anesthesia, so you feel no pain during the collection. Post-procedure soreness is common but manageable.

Generally, donors must be between 18 and 44 years old and in good health. Specific eligibility criteria can vary by registry, so it's best to check with the organization you register with.
